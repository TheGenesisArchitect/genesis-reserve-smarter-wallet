Part 03 Q&A Preparation 20 questions · Study the hard ones first
Category A — Skeptic Questions
"15% APY sounds too good to be true. What's the catch?"
There is no hidden catch, but there is a risk profile. DeFi lending yields fluctuate. 15% is the Accelerate tier — the highest tier with the longest commitment. Preserve tier is 6%. Growth is 10%. The yield comes from real interest payments from borrowers using USDC for productive economic activity — leverage, arbitrage, institutional liquidity. If aggregate yields compress below 10%, the Accelerate tier adjusts. We communicate this transparently in advance.
"Who audited your smart contracts?"
We are in final audit with [firm — insert when complete]. We do not deploy AUM until the audit is complete and published. The audit scope covers StrategyRouter, yield settlement, treasury reinvestment, and insurance premium routing. Full audit report is made public. No hidden findings.
"What happens if a DeFi protocol in your routing gets hacked?"
StrategyRouter enforces a 25% maximum allocation per protocol via smart contract logic — it cannot be overridden. Worst-case exposure in a single-protocol exploit: 25% of routed AUM. We maintain a protocol reserve fund from treasury reinvestment specifically for this scenario. Smart contract insurance through Nexus Mutual or equivalent is being evaluated as an additional hedge.
"USDC is controlled by Circle. What if Circle fails?"
Circle holds $321.9B in US Treasuries and cash equivalents held at BNY Mellon and in BlackRock money market funds. If Circle collapsed, reserve assets would be distributed to USDC holders. This is a systemic financial risk — the same risk applies to PayPal, Venmo, Chase. USDC is arguably safer than bank deposits, which are only insured to $250K per account.
"This sounds like a Ponzi — early depositors paid by new depositors."
Yield is not paid by new depositor capital. It is generated by lending USDC to creditworthy DeFi protocols (Aave, Compound, Morpho) who pay interest to borrow stable capital. The interest comes from real borrowers. This is identical to how a bank works — deposits are lent, and the interest is shared. The only difference: we share far more of it. 10–15% vs. 0.01–0.5% at Chase.
Category B — Technical Questions
"How exactly does StrategyRouter decide where to allocate?"
Every 15 minutes, StrategyRouter calculates for each of the 7 corridors: (a) current APY, (b) risk score (protocol TVL, audit history, exploit rate), (c) liquidity depth, and (d) gas cost of reallocation. It then solves for maximum risk-adjusted yield subject to constraints: no corridor > 25%, minimum liquidity threshold, and a capital buffer for instant withdrawals. All on-chain, on Arbitrum, gas costs in cents per epoch.
"What are the seven yield corridors?"
Aave V3, Compound V3, Morpho Blue (peer-to-peer efficiency), Pendle (yield tokenization), Curve/Convex (stablecoin AMM yields), direct USDC lending to creditworthy counterparties, and a reserve buffer in T-bill-backed yield products (Ondo Finance, Superstate). New corridors are added via governance vote after a security review period.
"How do you handle the insurance premium routing technically?"
Each insured user has a premium reserve balance. After each epoch, the settlement layer calculates the pro-rata premium due (annual premium ÷ 365 ÷ 96), routes that micro-allocation to a premium escrow contract. Escrow accumulates until reaching the monthly/quarterly threshold, then executes an off-chain payment via ACH or wire to the carrier. This is our settlement layer, not the DeFi routing layer.
"What chain are you on? What are the bridging risks?"
Arbitrum One. Gas fees are cents, finality under 1 second, deepest DeFi liquidity outside Ethereum mainnet. Critically: there is no cross-chain bridge in the user deposit flow. User funds move from their bank or wallet directly to the Arbitrum vault. No bridging exposure in the core product.
Category C — Regulatory Questions
"Are you a registered investment advisor? Is this securities law compliant?"
Genesis Reserve does not offer investment advice or discretionary management. Users select their own tier. The yield is structured as interest income — not a return on a security. We have obtained a legal opinion confirming the USDC vault product does not constitute a security under current SEC guidance. Insurance is offered via licensed IMO partnership. We are in ongoing dialogue with legal counsel as the regulatory landscape evolves.
"What about KYC/AML compliance?"
Full KYC/AML at onboarding via Privy embedded wallets with identity verification. We comply with FinCEN requirements as a money services business. Users from OFAC-sanctioned jurisdictions are blocked at wallet creation. SARs filed as required. Compliance is the price of operating at institutional scale — we built for it from day one.
"Insurance is highly regulated. How are you selling it without a license?"
We are not the producer of record in Phase 1. We are a technology platform that refers users to licensed insurance professionals through our IMO partner. The IMO holds the licenses. Licensed agents issue the policies. Genesis earns a technology platform referral fee — a well-established, compliant model. In Phase 2 and 3, we acquire direct licenses or carrier partnerships. A planned regulatory evolution, not a gap.
Category D — Business Model & Competition
"What stops Aave or Compound from building this directly?"
Aave and Compound are lending protocols — pipes, not products. Building a consumer product requires KYC, wallet UX, debit card, remittance, insurance partnerships, and customer support. None of that is in their mandate, team composition, or token holder incentive structure. More importantly, they cannot offer yield-funded insurance without a licensed insurance relationship, which requires building an entirely different business. They are our infrastructure. We route through them.
"What stops JPMorgan from doing this?"
Structural prevention. Regulatory constraints prevent large banks from holding DeFi protocol exposure. Their compliance teams require 3–5 years to approve a product like this. Their technology cannot settle 96 times per day across 7 protocols. And critically: they earn $0 on USDC deposits held at the Fed. We earn 10–15%. The incentive to build this does not exist inside the traditional banking system.
"What is your defensible moat against a better-funded copycat?"
Four layers: (1) Insurance carrier relationships take 12–18 months to establish. (2) A user with an active IUL policy has near-zero incentive to leave — they cannot bring their policy. (3) Larger AUM = deeper corridor liquidity = better yield = more users — this compounds. (4) Our compliance infrastructure costs $2–5M and 18+ months to replicate — it is itself a moat.
Category E — Investor Questions
"What are your key risks and how do you mitigate them?"
Risk 1 — DeFi yield compression: Multi-corridor routing eliminates single protocol dependency. Tiers adjust transparently. Risk 2 — Smart contract exploit: 25% per-corridor cap, protocol reserve fund, smart contract insurance. Risk 3 — Regulatory shift: Modular architecture — insurance layer can be switched off in adverse jurisdictions without affecting vault yield product. Risk 4 — USDC depeg: User disclosure — same risk as holding USDC in any wallet. Not a Genesis structural risk.
"Why should I believe the 40% insurance adoption rate?"
It is a design outcome, not an assumption. When insurance costs the user $0 and the alternative is having no life insurance at all, the question becomes: why would a rational person decline? The $0-premium framing flips the default. Traditional insurance has low penetration because premiums compete with rent and lifestyle. On Genesis, the premium is paid before the user sees the yield. There is no trade-off. We target 40% as our conservative model assumption.
"How do you make money in year one before scale?"
Year 1 revenue is yield spread: the 2% difference between DeFi APY captured (~12%) and APY paid to users (~10%). On $10M AUM: $200K. Not the story. The story is the structural economics that activate at $100M+ AUM. IMO referral commissions also contribute in year one — each insured user generates a first-year commission. At 5,000 insured users, that is meaningful year-one insurance revenue while we build toward scale.

Breakout A The Technology Deep Dive Engineers · CTOs · Technical investors

StrategyRouter Architecture

A Solidity smart contract on Arbitrum One. Every 900 seconds, Chainlink Automation triggers routeEpoch():

routeEpoch() logic: 1. Read current allocations from each registered protocol adapter 2. Query current APYs via each protocol's oracle 3. Calculate risk-adjusted yield per corridor (weighted score) 4. Optimize: maximize Σ(allocation[i] × APY[i]) Constraints: allocation[i] ≤ 25%, liquidity_buffer ≥ MIN_BUFFER 5. Execute reallocation transactions via protocol adapters 6. Emit epoch events for settlement layer

Protocol adapters are modular. Adding a corridor requires deploying a new adapter contract + governance whitelist. No core contract changes needed.

Insurance Premium Routing

Off-chain settlement layer, not DeFi routing. Each insured user has a premiumReserve balance. After each epoch:

premiumReserve += epochYield × premiumAllocationRatio When premiumReserve ≥ scheduledPremiumAmount: → Execute ACH/wire payment to carrier → Log policy payment event on-chain

The user's vault balance never decreases. Premium comes from the yield spread — the difference between gross DeFi yield and net APY paid to the user.

Privy + Wallet Architecture

  • @privy-io/react-auth v3.27.1 — authentication via email, SMS, or social login
  • @privy-io/wagmi v4.0.8 — wallet-to-wagmi bridge
  • Arbitrum One as default chain. USDC deposit flow: Bank → Circle ACH → USDC → Privy wallet → Genesis vault contract
  • Security model: Genesis never holds private keys. Privy MPC key management. No single party can unilaterally access user funds.
Breakout B The Insurance Deep Dive Insurance professionals · Wealth managers · Financial advisors

IUL Mechanics

Two accounts in every IUL policy: (1) Term Cost of Insurance — covers pure death benefit risk. (2) Accumulation Account — the cash value.

Index Credit = min(cap, max(floor, index_return × participation_rate)) Example: S&P returns +18%, cap = 11%, floor = 0% → Credit = min(11%, max(0%, 18%)) = 11% ← Capped upside Example: S&P returns -25%, floor = 0% → Credit = max(0%, -25%) = 0% ← Zero downside
